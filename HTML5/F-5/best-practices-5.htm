How to time and pace your text slides

The timing and pacing of text slides can play a crucial role in the navigability of web resources. Those with blindness, low vision, dexterity impairments, and cognitive limitations (refer to section 5.1). So, designing with this in mind could greatly improve the experience of users watching your video.

In this case, including a transcript may be beneficial for users to refer back to at their own pace. Additionally, lengthening the time slides are displayed or allowing users to adjust the slide duration would be beneficial, as it would give them more time to process the information.

For example, if you are presenting a video presentation to a classroom full of students, most will be reading at different paces. So, lengthening the time for which the slide is displayed will account for those who may take a little longer to read (and take notes).

It is best practice to avoid using excessive transitions as they can be extremely distracting for users. Government resources suggest that sticking to transitions involving minimal movement (like sliding motions) and avoiding unnecessary transitions is most effective.

How to address flashing, motion, and strobe lighting in your video: guidelines and best practices

It is important to first consider seriously whether the video requires flashing, motion, and strobe to meet its goals, as the presence of these elements poses a severe threat. If these elements are deemed necessary, WCAG provides detailed guidelines to help us ensure that any flashing lights, images, or strobe lighting are anticipates the needs of those with epilepsy, migraines, or autism (see section 5.1).

These flashing lights can be categorized as:

  • General flash: WCAG defines this as “a pair of opposing changes in relative luminance of 10% or more of the maximum relative luminance (1.0) where the relative luminance of the darker image is below 0.80; and where 'a pair of opposing changes' is an increase followed by a decrease, or a decrease followed by an increase.” This essentially means that general flash is a light that flashes in quick succession.

  • Red light: This is defined as “any pair of opposing transitions involving a saturated red” (WCAG). This type of flash ( a flash colored red) is more likely to trigger seizures in people with photosensitive epilepsy due to its longer wavelengths.

Regardless of the type of flash, it is best practice to observe the three flashes or below threshold–which requires web creators and designers to ensure that there are no more than three flashes in any one second period (WCAG level AAA). While WCAG level A states that ensuring that “the combined area of flashes occurring concurrently occupies no more than a total of .006 steradians within any 10 degree visual field on the screen (25% of any 10 degree visual field on the screen) at typical viewing distance” also reduces the risk of seizures.

For example, if your video includes a segment with a flashing image for a second, then you, as the video editor, must ensure that the image is not flashing more than three times in that second. Consider first if the flash is necessary, or if it is merely decorative.

Checking the accessibility of your transitions

There are a few ways to verify that your videos pass the WCAG guidelines on flashing lights in your videos.

Testing manually:

You can decrease the video playback speed and count the number of flashes per second, ensuring that the flash count per second falls within the three flash threshold.

Using automated tools:

Testing manually may be sufficient for short one-off transitions, but tools may be helpful for analyzing videos with multiple transitions, or longer durations of potentially harmful content. Note that it is important to keep WCAG guidelines in mind if you choose to use these tools.

  • HardingFPA is a commercial analysis tool used by broadcasters or video game content developers. You can purchase analyses of your video on a per-video basis.

  • Flicker Filter is a Chrome extension that detects photosensitive stimuli and uses machine learning to classify videos as either safe or harmful to viewers with epilepsy.

  • Video Flashing Reduction Tool is an open-source tool released by Apple for video creators to detect flashing lights in their videos.

  • PhotosensitivityPal is a Chrome extension designed for users with epilepsy that scans videos in real-time and blocks content that it detects as potentially harmful, but may be used by video creators to test their content.
