Approaches to accessibility

Accessibility is vital to ensure that our writing and products can reach as many people as possible. With that in mind, there are several approaches you can use when it comes to implementing accessibility within your work. This page breaks all the different approaches into two designs: accessible and universal design. Understanding how these two approaches differ will improve how you create content while accounting for accessibility.

Accessible design

Typical accessible design is an approach which directly addresses accessibility for people with disabilities. However, accessible design sometimes results in products which consider accessibility as an afterthought. For example, accessible design might place stairs at the main entrance and place ramps in unappealing locations, such as in the back of the building, which makes accessibility possible but less inviting. The main priority of accessible design is that there is “some form of access” (Kalbag 2017) rather than the most optimized and fluid integration of accessibility. A technical writer using accessible design might create a function on a website to increase text size, but the setting might be hidden or difficult to find. While accessibility is technically a part of the website, it’s not entirely comfortable to use.

Universal design

Accessibility should be approached with the universal design method. Universal design is an approach to accessibility that considers the entire, broader clientele, which includes both people with disabilities and people with able bodies, seeking to create a cohesive and equitable infrastructure. The most important difference is how universal design creates websites and products which are accessible to everybody, “which includes, but isn’t limited to, people with disabilities” (Kalbag 2017). A building utilizing universal design might integrate a ramp/stair combination at the front entrance which both people with disabilities and people without disabilities can use with ease. In practice, a technical writer might use universal design when creating a website by making the text larger so anybody with visual impairments can read it.

Work Cited

Kalbag, Laura. “Considering Accessibility.” Accessibility for Everyone, A Book Apart, 2017.
