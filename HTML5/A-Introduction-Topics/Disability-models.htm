Disibility models

In order to optimize accessibility for all users, as a technical writer you should consider the actual definition of disability and how different approaches to disability can influence accessibility. There are two methods or “models” which determine how people can view disabilities. Depending on which model you choose to view disabilities, you might unknowingly create products that aren’t accessible for a wide range of people.

Medical model

The medical model of disability observes disabilities through a statistical lens. It is best used in clinical settings, utilized by doctors and policymakers who aren’t necessarily seeking accessibility. The medical model is categorical; either someone is disabled or they aren’t. As a result, when used by inventors and technical writers without the input of disabled users, this model can result in approaches to accessibility which do not actually consider the needs of its audience. For example, a company might create bulky gloves that deaf people can wear to interpret their signing to speech or text that in practice is too “unwieldy, unstable, uncomfortable and/or expensive to be usable” (May 2020). For a doctor, the medical model is necessary to diagnose and help treat certain disabilities. However, as a method to approach accessibility for all users, this model falls short.

Social model

Due to the limitations of the medical model, technical writers should consider an alternative, the social model. The social model believes in both “the shortcomings of society in respect of disability, and the abilities and capabilities of people with disabilities themselves” (May 2020). This model relies on working with our audience to seek the most accessible and comfortable experience. Focus groups and working with users lead to more products which appeal to everyone. It is our responsibility as technical writers and content creators to design websites and services that are accessible to everybody regardless of disability, gender, race, and all the other ways we differ from each other. Truly inclusive design includes anybody and benefits everybody.
