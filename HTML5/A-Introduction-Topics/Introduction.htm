Introduction

People with disabilities should not have to go out of their way to be a part of our world. Society should not punish people with disabilities for needing accommodations that we failed to account for. For this reason, accessibility should always be in a technical writer’s mind when creating their content. We seek an optimal and comfortable experience for all users of our products regardless of any differences or accommodations they might require.

Why accessibility matters

Accessibility benefits everybody, not just people with disabilities. In recent times, a lot of assistive technology has been used not only by people with disabilities but by most people. Closed captions and voice assistants like Alexa or Siri are just a couple of products or inventions created with the intent of accommodating people with disabilities which ended up being popularized and widely used by a variety of users, some without any disabilities. With the rampant rise of such technologies, it has become increasingly more important for technical writers and website creators to keep accessibility and disabilities in mind. Understanding how to best integrate accessibility when designing websites, documents, and software to not exclude should be our ultimate goal as technical writers.

Accessibility, as defined in this draft, refers to the degree to which the world is acclimated to the needs of everybody, including both people with and without disabilities. Web accessibility refers to how acclimated a specific website is to the needs of everybody. A wide range of disabilities might impact how a user interacts with a website and it's important to consider screen readers and other assistive technologies which people with disabilities might rely on. Implementing accessibility for everyone ensure that our products reach as many people as possible.
