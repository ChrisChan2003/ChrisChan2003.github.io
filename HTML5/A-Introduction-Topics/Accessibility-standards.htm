Accessibility standards

Determining how accessible certain content might be can be difficult for creators without any disabilities themselves. While working together with your audience through a social model lens is an ideal method to achieving universal design and addressing accessibility while designing your own products, there are some standards set in place by the law. Different policies and laws relating to disability have emerged in recent times. For technical writers, an especially important one to follow is the W3C/WAI Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).

What is WCAG

The W3C/WAI Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are what policies pertainnining to accessibility online are based on. These guidelines address web accessibility by setting “a minimum acceptable level of service to people with disabilities” (May 2020). With these policies and guidelines in place, website developers and online content creators must ensure that their products are truly inclusive and accessible to everybody, including people with disabilities. WCAG is important for technical writers especially, who work to create content so that a vast majority of people can use it. While WCAG and the policies surrounding accessibility promote a minimum threshold for what makes a product accessible, our job should be to go beyond the bottom line. WCAG is a great starting point for accessibility, and technical writers should base their work around it. However, it is equally important to pursue inclusivity and accessibility to the utmost degree, creating designs and products which can be used by anybody and everybody regardless of any differences in ability.
