What is cognitive load?

Cognitive load refers to the amount of information our working memory can process at any time. In other words, when our brains get overloaded, they may struggle to function.

This might mean:

  • Our performance may suffer

  • We might take longer to process information and overlook details

  • We may even give up on a task

In the context of digital experiences, reducing cognitive load is essential. While visually appealing design and engaging content are important, they are not enough on their own – the key is keeping the user’s mental load down.

A truly effective website prioritizes usability and accessibility by minimizing the mental effort required to interact with it. This means thinking about being intentional with design choices, and including appropriate accessibility features.

How does cognitive load relate to accessibility?

Cognitive disabilities can encompass a broad range of conditions that may require additional accessibility measures to lighten mental strain.

These conditions can impair functions such as:

  • Memory

  • Attention

  • Perception

  • Learning

  • Problem Solving

  • Comprehension

These conditions can be permanent, such as those who have ADHD, but can also be temporary or situational, such as having a concussion or brain injury – but, even individuals without cognitive disabilities often find themselves overloaded. Factors like illness, fatigue, stress, or distraction can impair anyone’s cognitive function, despite their cognitive health.

For many users, reducing cognitive load is a nice plus, but for those with disabilities, how accessible a digital environment is determines whether they can access the digital content at all.

How can assistive technology be used to reduce cognitive load?

Assistive technology devices (ATs), both hardware and software, are designed to help those with disabilities perform tasks more effectively. Examples include screen readers and navigation aids.

However, ATs are only effective if web developers implement them thoughtfully and ensure their functionality.

Take for example: captions and transcripts for videos.

These ATs may help reduce cognitive load for those with disabilities like:

  • Auditory Processing Disorder, a disorder in which the brain struggles to interpret auditory information

  • Attention-deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, characterized by persistent patterns of inattention and hyperactivity

But, poor implementation of captions and transcripts can actually increase cognitive load and make the user even more frustrated. These issues can include:

  • Unsynchronized captions

  • Small or hard-to-read text

  • Difficult-to-find controls

  • Misspellings or inaccuracies

  • Lack of playback controls (pause/rewind)

These accessibility issues can render these ATs completely unusable.

When developers overlook these crucial components of web content creation, people with disabilities become especially vulnerable to cognitive strain, which can hinder their ability to process information effectively.

By prioritizing these considerations when designing videos, digital accessibility becomes more effective, allowing users to engage with content more comfortably.
