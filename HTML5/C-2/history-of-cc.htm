History of closed captions

Captioning has transformed the way many people consume media, especially those who are deaf or hard of hearing. Decades of effort and innovation went into developing closed captions as we know today, helping make television and digital video accessible to a wider audience.

1971

  • ABC worked with the National Bureau of Standards (NBS) to experiment with transmitting information, inspiring the idea to send captions enclosed in the television signal.

  • Debuting potential captioning technologies, the first National Conference on Television for the Hearing Impaired was held

    Image from Tennessee State Museum on Facebook: https://www.facebook.com/share/1CN3Ytip9f/
1972

  • NBS proves the viability of closed captions by embedding them in a regular broadcast.

  • The first open captioned program aired, meaning captions appeared for everyone and could not be turned off.

1973

  • ABC began broadcasting World News Tonight with open captions as the only timely newscast accessible to the deaf and hard of hearing audience.

  • Funds from the federal government allowed for testing of closed captioning which PBS developed under contract to the Bureau of Education for the Handicapped.

    Image from GBH: https://www.wgbh.org/foundation/services/ncam/about-ncam/access-for-all-major-milestones-in-media-accessibility
1976

  • Line 21 of the television signal was reserved by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) for the transmission of closed captions.

1979

  • With a mission to promote and provide access to television programs, the National Captioning Institute (NCI) was established.

1980

  • Decoder boxes, mechanisms needed to view captions, went on sale to the public.

  • The Sears TeleCaption adapter was the first decoder that allowed viewers to bring closed captions into their home.

    Image from the Henry Ford Museum: https://www.thehenryford.org/collections/explore/artifact/328044
1982

  • NCI provided the first real-time captioning for the Academy Awards, a live event.

1990

  • President George H.W. Bush signed a bill mandating all televisions over 13 inches to be capable of displaying closed captions sold after July 1, 1993.

  • The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) is passed, prohibiting discrimination in employment, state and local government services.

2012

  • Closed captioning requirements for programming delivered through the Internet were established by the FCC, guaranteeing a universal level of accessibility in electronics.
